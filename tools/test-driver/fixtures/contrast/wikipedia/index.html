Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and certain bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in the bonds of sugar molecules. It is, in a very real sense, the engine that powers nearly every food chain on the planet, and the source of the oxygen that most living things depend on to breathe.

The reactions take place largely within specialized structures called chloroplasts, which contain the green pigment chlorophyll. Chlorophyll absorbs light most strongly in the blue and red parts of the visible spectrum while reflecting green, which is why most leaves appear the color they do to the human eye.

Overview

Although the overall equation is often written as a single tidy line, the underlying biochemistry unfolds in two linked stages. The first captures energy from sunlight; the second uses that energy to assemble sugars from carbon dioxide drawn out of the air.

  • The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes.
  • The light-independent reactions, or Calvin cycle, occur in the stroma.
  • Water is split, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.
  • Carbon dioxide is fixed into organic carbon compounds.

History

Our modern understanding was assembled piece by piece over several centuries, beginning with simple experiments that weighed a growing plant against the soil it grew in. Each generation of researchers added a layer, and the full picture only came into focus once chemists could trace individual atoms through the reaction with radioactive labels.
