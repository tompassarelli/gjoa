Engineers build a battery that charges in seconds and lasts for years

The new electrode chemistry could reshape how grid-scale storage is deployed across the country, researchers say, by cutting both the cost and the charging time of large lithium packs without sacrificing safety.

A decade in the making

For the past ten years a small group of materials scientists has been quietly chasing a stubborn problem: how to move ions through a solid electrolyte fast enough to be useful without cracking the material apart. The answer, it turns out, was hiding in the geometry of the crystal lattice rather than in any exotic new element.

The team published its findings this week, and outside experts have already begun calling the result one of the more consequential storage advances in recent memory. If the manufacturing process scales, the same approach could find its way into everything from electric vehicles to backyard solar installations within a few years.

Still, the researchers are careful to temper expectations. Lab demonstrations rarely survive contact with a factory floor unchanged, and the cost of the specialized coating remains an open question. The group plans to spend the next phase of the project working with industrial partners to find out whether the chemistry holds up at volume.
